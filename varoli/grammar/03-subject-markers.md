# 03 — Subject Markers

Subject markers are single vowels that precede a verb to indicate the perspective from which the action is experienced. They do not modify the verb. They frame it.

Each vowel defines a perspective that corresponds to its elemental substrate. The subject marker answers: from whose position is this verb being experienced?

For the elemental definitions of each vowel, see `../core/02-vowels.md`.

---

## The Five Subject Markers

---

### e — Self (Ego)

The perspective of the individuated self. The bounded, distinct identity experiencing or performing the verb.

**Usage:** `e vi` — self is present in the field.

---

### a — Collective (Nos)

The perspective of the relational medium. The shared, collective experience of the verb. Not a specific group but the substrate of relation itself.

**Usage:** `a va` — the collective flows as relation.

---

### i — Universal (Omnia)

The perspective of the condition of appearance. The verb experienced from the position of the field itself — not any located self but the condition in which all selves appear.

**Usage:** `i vi` — the field itself is present. The condition of appearance, as its own subject.

---

### o — Other (Tu)

The perspective of stable addressable form. The verb experienced from the position of a specific, located, bounded other.

**Usage:** `o vo` — the other exists as form.

---

### u — Unnamed (Between)

The perspective of undifferentiated origin. The verb experienced from a position prior to identity, relation, field, or form. The between-one. The dissolving.

**Usage:** `u vu` — the unnamed exists as origin.

---

## Structural Rules

1. The subject marker is always a single vowel.
2. The subject marker always precedes the verb.
3. The subject marker does not modify the verb. It identifies the perspective from which the verb is experienced. `e va` and `a va` contain the same verb (*va*: being-as-relation) but are experienced from different positions — self versus collective.
4. The subject marker corresponds to the pronoun system derived from the elemental palindromes: *e* from *eYe*, *a* from *aVa*, *i* from *iLi*, *o* from *oRo*, *u* from *uMu*.
5. Subject markers apply to all verb sets — attractor, operator, and general. The marker system is independent of which consonant class produces the verb.

---

## Notes

The five subject markers and the five vowels are the same characters. This is not coincidence. In Varoli, who you are is which element you are speaking from. The subject is not a grammatical role assigned to a noun. It is the elemental substrate that frames the experience.

This means Varoli has no third-person perspective in the English sense. Every sentence is experienced from a substrate. `o` is not "he/she/they" — it is the perspective of bounded form itself. The distinction is ontological, not referential.
