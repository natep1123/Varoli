# 05 — Primitive Nouns

A primitive noun in Varoli is a palindromic VCV structure: a vowel, a consonant or operator at center, and the same vowel repeated. The noun reads from center outward. The center character is the attractor — the force or event held in balance. The surrounding vowel is the domain that holds it.

Palindromic closure is a hard requirement for nouns. Non-palindromic VCV forms have directionality — they lean — and leaning is a verb property. A noun has balance. It reads the same from both directions. It is a thing, not a motion.

This document covers the 25 primitive palindromic nouns produced by the four attractor consonants (v, l, r, m) and the operator (y) across the five elemental domains. General consonant nouns follow the same pattern and are documented separately.

For the invariant function of each primitive, see `../core/05-phonemic-schemas.md`. For generation rules governing palindromic forms, see `../core/06-generation-rules.md`.

---

## Reading Principle

Every palindromic noun reads from center outward:

1. Identify the center character — this is the attractor
2. Read outward — the surrounding vowel is the domain
3. The noun names the stable thing that exists when this force is held within this domain

The typographic system marks the center character with capitalization, making the attractor visible before the word is parsed.

---

## Two Categories of Primitive Noun

The 25 primitive nouns divide into two structural categories:

**Attractor palindromes (20 forms):** A consonant force held in balance within a domain. These name things that persist because a force is held steady. Four of these are native pairings where the attractor meets its own element (aVa, iLi, oRo, uMu). Sixteen are cross-domain forms where an attractor is held within a non-native element.

**Operator palindromes (5 forms):** The collapse event held in balance within a domain. These name things that persist because a moment of becoming is itself stabilized. One of these is the native pairing where the operator meets the element whose nature is inherently a collapse event (eYe). Four are cross-domain forms.

The five native pairings form a diagonal across the grid. But they are not all the same kind of pairing. The four attractor diagonals (aVa, iLi, oRo, uMu) are force meeting its own element — the consonant that makes the element real, held within that element. The fire diagonal (eYe) is the operator meeting the element whose nature is inherently a collapse event — individuation is the most structurally visible outcome of superposition resolving. These are different structural relationships that produce the same form type. Fire's native palindrome uses the operator rather than an attractor consonant because fire is the element of becoming, and becoming is what the operator does.

Where the attractor palindromes name things that persist because a force is held steady, the operator palindromes name things that persist because a collapse event is held steady. These are nouns of threshold — stable forms built around an inherently instantaneous event.

The operator's positional behavior at palindrome center is documented in `../core/05-phonemic-schemas.md`: "At the center of a palindrome: marks the attractor state as the collapse event itself. The center is not a substance but an event."

## Structural Rules for Primitive Nouns

1. A primitive noun is a palindromic VCV structure.
2. The center character is the attractor. It is typographically marked with capitalization.
3. The surrounding vowel is the domain.
4. The noun reads from center outward.
5. Palindromic closure is required. Non-palindromic VCV forms are not nouns.
6. Every combination of center (4 attractors + 1 operator) and domain (5 vowels) produces a valid primitive noun.
7. Native pairings (attractor meeting its own element, or operator meeting fire) are the elemental palindromes. Cross-domain pairings are derived nouns.
8. No two primitive nouns overlap. Each names a structurally distinct stable thing.

## Notes

The 25 primitive nouns documented here use only the attractor consonants and the operator. General consonant palindromes (using the 17 general consonants) follow the same structural pattern and are documented separately. The full primitive + general noun space is 110 true palindromic VCV forms.

The five-character palindromic noun pattern (V1-C1-C2-C1-V1), proven by *arOva*, extends the noun space to 2,310 additional forms. That pattern and its constraints are documented in a separate lexicon document.

The doubled-center palindrome (V1-CC-V1) is flagged as an open question. It is excluded from active use until doubled consonant semantics are structurally defined.

The poetic field labels in this document (the living current, the fossil, the undertow, etc.) are mnemonic handles, not definitions. The structural readings are the authority.
