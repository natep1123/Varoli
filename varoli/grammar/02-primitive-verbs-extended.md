# 02 — General Primitive Verbs

This document extends the primitive verb system to the seventeen general consonants. Each general consonant follows the same CV/VC generation pattern established in `./01-primitive-verbs.md`: force projects into domain (CV, active), domain expresses through force (VC, receptive).

The attractor and operator sets (v, l, r, m, y) are documented in `./01-primitive-verbs.md` and are not repeated here. This document covers only the general set.

For the invariant function and full phonemic definition of each consonant, see `../core/05-phonemic-schemas.md`. For the generation rules governing valid CV and VC structures, see `../core/06-generation-rules.md`.

---

## Structural Notes

### General consonants vs attractor consonants

General consonants do not carry elemental affinity. They have no native domain. Unlike the attractor sets, where one verb per set is a root form (the force meeting its own element), the general consonant sets have no root form. All five verbs in each set are equally fundamental.

### Mode label asymmetries

Several general consonant sets use mode labels that depart from the default pattern. The principle established in the attractor sets applies here: the mode label follows the directional bias of the consonant, not a default template. The following sets carry asymmetric labels:

- d-set: "resisting-within" — d's bias is inward and opposing
- f-set: "releasing-from" — f's bias is outward and dispersing
- g-set: "gathering-into" — g's bias is inward and convergent
- k-set: "breaking-into" — k's bias is forceful threshold crossing
- b-set: "emerging-into" — b's bias is outward against resistance

These are the same class of derivable asymmetry as the m-set's "descending-into" documented in `./01-primitive-verbs.md`. Each is determined by the consonant's directional bias, not by inconsistency.

### CV/VC directionality

The directionality encoding principle applies to all general consonant sets. CV faces inward (arrival, entry). VC faces outward (departure, exit). For the full statement of this principle and its application in the diving system, see `./01-primitive-verbs.md`, section: Directionality Encoding.

#### Threshold Pair Note

*c* and *k* share a root function (threshold crossing) but differ in intensity. *c* is the laminar crossing, the transition that flows. *k* is the turbulent crossing, the transition that breaks. Using *c* where *k* is needed produces a crossing that fails. Using *k* where *c* is needed produces unnecessary rupture. The distinction is structural: did the crossing flow or did it break?

## Notes

The general consonant verbs follow the same structural principles as the attractor and operator verbs. All generation rules apply. All directionality encoding applies. The CV/VC distinction is active/receptive for all general consonant sets.

The general consonant verbs are not primitive in the same structural sense as the attractor verbs. The attractor consonants carry elemental affinity — their verb sets describe modes of existence native to a specific substrate. The general consonants are applied forces — their verb sets describe modes of interaction that move between, across, and through substrates. Both categories are valid CV/VC forms under the generation rules. The structural distinction is in the relationship between the consonant and the domains it enters.

Additional verb complexity (tense, aspect, completion) is built on these roots through consonant suffixes and is documented separately.
