# 01 — Primitive Verbs

Primitive verbs are CV structures in which a force (consonant) expresses into an elemental substrate (vowel). They do not describe actions upon objects. They describe modes of existence or states of being within a given element.

Every primitive verb answers one question: how does this force exist within this element?

The CV direction is primary. Force projects into element. The VC inversion (element projecting through force) is the reception layer and is documented separately.

This document covers the four attractor sets (v, l, r, m) and the operator set (y). The attractor sets each produce five verbs by projecting a single attractor force across all five elemental substrates. The operator set produces five verbs by projecting the collapse event across the same five substrates. Together these yield twenty-five primitive verbs. The attractor sets describe modes of existence. The operator set describes modes of becoming.

For the function and poetic definitions of each primitive, see `02-vowels.md` and `03-consonants.md` in `varoli/core/`. For generation rules governing valid CV structures, see `06-generation-rules.md`.

## The Four Attractor Sets

Each attractor consonant has a fixed quality of motion. Each vowel has a fixed elemental substrate. The primitive verb is what results when the force meets the element.

The four attractor consonants produce four complete sets of five verbs each:

| Set | Force | Quality | Mode |
|-----|-------|---------|------|
| v-set | vitality | self-originating projection | being-as-... |
| l-set | continuum | bidirectional threading | persisting-as-... |
| r-set | resonance | internal oscillation | resonating-as-... |
| m-set | depth | inward descent | descending-into-... |

Each set has a root form where the consonant meets the substrate of its own elemental affinity. The root form is the most natural expression of that force: the force doing what it does most fundamentally.

## The v-Set: Self-Originating Projection

*v* = self-originating outward projection. Force that initiates from an internal source rather than as a result of external cause.

Each entry below describes what it means for self-originating projection to express into a specific elemental domain.

### The Root Verb: vi

*vi* is the root form of to be in Varoli. It is not the only form of being but it is the most fundamental: self-originating projection into the universal field. To be is to project outward into the condition that allows appearance. Every other form of being is a more specific version of this.

When a speaker says *vi* they are not claiming individuation (*ve*), relation (*va*), form (*vo*), or return to origin (*vu*). They are claiming the most minimal possible mode of existence: presence within the field. The root of being is contribution to the everything.

This is why *vi* translates most naturally as *to be* or *to exist* while the other forms require longer phrases in English. English has one word for existence. Varoli has five, each pointing to a structurally different relationship between the force of self-origination and the domain into which it projects.

## The m-Set: Depth

*m* = inward descending force. Movement toward interior states, hidden layers, and conditions beneath the surface of what is immediately perceptible. The force of submersion and concealment.

Where the v-set describes how a force exists within a domain, the m-set describes how a force descends into a domain. The mode labels in this set use "descending-into" rather than "being-as." This reflects the directional bias of *m* (inward, descending) more honestly than "being-as" would. The asymmetry is intentional and derivable from the consonant's function. Depth does not project outward, thread across, or vibrate within. It pulls inward. The mode label follows the force.

## The Complete Primitive Verb System

The twenty-five primitive verbs describe the complete range of primitive modes available across the system:

| | a (relation) | e (individuation) | i (field) | o (form) | u (origin) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **v** (projection) | va | ve | vi | vo | vu |
| **l** (continuum) | la | le | li | lo | lu |
| **r** (resonance) | ra | re | ri | ro | ru |
| **m** (depth) | ma | me | mi | mo | mu |
| **y** (collapse) | ya | ye | yi | yo | yu |

Four attractor forces and one operator. Five domains. Twenty-five verbs. No overlap.

The attractor sets (v, l, r, m) describe modes of existence: being, persisting, resonating, descending. The operator set (y) describes modes of becoming: the event of a domain crystallizing from superposition. These are two structurally distinct verb categories sharing the same CV generation pattern.

The four attractor root verbs:

| Root | Set | Translation Handle |
|------|-----|--------------------|
| vi | v-set | to be |
| li | l-set | to endure |
| ro | r-set | to resound |
| mu | m-set | to descend |

The y-set has no root form. The operator has no elemental affinity.

The English handles are loose approximations, not definitions. The structural definitions are the authority.

## The VC Inversions (Reception Layer)

Each CV form has a VC inversion in which the element projects through the force rather than the force projecting into the element:

| CV form | VC inversion | Shift |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| CV: force → element | VC: element → force | active → receptive |

This applies across all four attractor sets. The VC inversions represent the reception layer: not what the force does to the element but what the element does through the force. The full set of attractor VC inversions is documented separately.

The y-set inversions (ay, ey, iy, oy, uy) are structurally distinct from the attractor inversions. For the attractor sets, the CV/VC distinction maps onto active/receptive. For the y-set, the CV/VC distinction maps onto collapse/dissolution: the y-verb (CV) describes the moment a domain crystallizes from superposition, while the y-inversion (VC) describes the moment a domain releases back into superposition. This asymmetry is derivable from the operator's unique structural position — *y* is not a force with a direction but an event with two phases. The y-inversions are documented alongside the attractor inversions in the inversions document.

### Directionality Encoding

The CV/VC distinction encodes more than grammatical voice. It encodes structural directionality: orientation toward center or away from it.

- CV (force → element): arrival, entry, descent, compression toward center
- VC (element → force): departure, exit, ascent, expansion away from center

This is already operative in the Yemu diving system. The zone declarations are palindromic precisely because they contain both directions simultaneously. *av-e-va* encodes both arrival (*av*: element receiving the diver) and departure (*va*: the diver projecting back into the element). The direction changes. The word does not.

The gate codes make this explicit. In *av-y-mu*, the left side (*av*) is VC — departure from the current domain. The right side (*mu*) is CV — arrival at the next domain. The operator *y* sits between them as the collapse event that separates release from crystallization. The gate code is the structural junction between departure and arrival, with directionality encoded in the morpheme order.

This means every primitive verb carries a built-in orientation. The CV form faces inward. The VC form faces outward. The palindrome holds both. See `yemu/applications/diving/01-domains.md` for the application of this principle in the diving mechanics.

## The y-Set: Operator Verbs (Collapse)

*y* = context-resolved collapse operator. The mechanism by which unresolved states become resolved. Not a force. Not a quality of motion. The event of resolution itself.

The y-set is structurally distinct from the four attractor sets. The attractor verbs describe modes of existence: how a force exists, persists, resonates, or descends within a domain. The operator is not a force. It does not exist within a domain. It is the event that determines which domain becomes real.

The y-verbs describe modes of becoming: the moment an unresolved state collapses into a specific domain. Not the state. The strike. Where the attractor verbs are thunder, the y-verbs are lightning.

The y-set is valid under generation rules. CV structure with the operator in consonant position is documented positional behavior in `05-phonemic-schemas.md`: "In consonant position: marks the active event of collapse within a structure. The moment potential becomes actual through structural resolution."

### No Root Form

The attractor sets each have a root form where the consonant meets the substrate of its own elemental affinity (vi, li, ro, mu). The y-set has no root form. The operator has no elemental affinity. It belongs to no domain. All five y-verbs are equally fundamental because collapse occurs across all domains without being native to any single one.

If any y-verb has structural privilege it is *ye*, because the relationship between *y* and *e* is the most documented in the system: *eYe* is the palindromic form of fire, the only element whose center is the operator rather than an attractor consonant. But this privilege belongs to fire's unique structural position, not to the operator having an affinity. *y* remains universal.

---

### Relationship to eYe

The y-verb *ye* is the verbal form of what *eYe* encodes as a noun. *eYe* is fire made real through the moment of collapse: individuation as a stable palindromic structure. *ye* is the event of that collapse occurring: the instant individuation becomes real. The noun is the completed architecture. The verb is the lightning that built it.

This structural correspondence confirms that the y-set is not an invention but a derivation. The relationship between the operator and fire was already present in the palindromic system. The y-verbs make it available as a verbal category.

## Notes

Primitive verbs are not conjugated through their root form. Tense, aspect, and subject are encoded through the vowel prefix and consonant suffix system documented in the grammar files. The primitive verb root remains stable across all inflections.

The five sets (v, l, r, m, y) establish the complete primitive verb inventory: twenty-five verbs across twenty-five unique structural positions. Additional verb sets using general consonants will follow the same CV structure and generation rules, producing modes of existence through their respective qualities of motion (threshold, compression, seeking, dissolution, and so on). These are not primitive in the same structural sense as the attractor and operator verbs but are valid derived forms.
