## 2. The main reading rule

Primitive directional forms should always be read from:

1. primitive class
2. invariant function
3. directionality
4. substrate effect

Do not jump straight to poetic gloss.

The most reliable reading sequence is:

- what is the force
- what is the domain
- is the form CV or VC
- what changes because of that direction

---

## 3. The most important asymmetry

### CV and VC are not mirrors in the ordinary sense

They are paired, but not interchangeable.

- **CV** = force arriving into domain
- **VC** = domain expressing through force

This means:
- `va` is not just a reversed spelling of `av`
- `ve` is not “the same thing from the other side”
- `mu` and `um` do not describe the same structural event

The inventory is easiest to misuse when CV and VC are treated as equivalent lexical alternants.
They are not.

---

## 4. Why the files are grouped by vowel

The directional-form folder is organized by substrate rather than by consonant because Varoli generation is easier to learn by domain than by force inventory alone.

This makes it easier to ask:
- what does this domain permit
- how does each force behave in this domain
- what kinds of worlds or scenes this substrate may anchor later

This grouping is especially useful for:
- storytelling
- scene-building
- conversational experiments
- stable-world generation

## 5. Attractor forms and non-attractor forms

The attractor consonants are:
- `v`
- `l`
- `r`
- `m`

These often feel more immediately “native” or stable because they connect directly to the canonical primitive stable worlds:
- `aVa`
- `iLi`
- `oRo`
- `uMu`
- and the operator-centered `eYe`

That does **not** mean the general consonants are secondary.

The general consonants are just as real.
They often carry:
- threshold behavior
- pressure
- contact
- seeking
- release
- cohesion
- oscillation
- recognition
- and other highly useful motion qualities

A common drift risk is to overvalue attractor forms and underuse the general set.
Do not do that.

## 6. A practical learnability pattern

The easiest way to learn the directional inventory is:

### Step 1
Learn the five substrates:
- `u`
- `i`
- `a`
- `e`
- `o`

### Step 2
Learn the attractor sets first:
- `v`
- `l`
- `r`
- `m`
- `y`

### Step 3
Then learn the general consonants in families:
- threshold pair
- pressure pair
- contact/resistance pair
- seeking/recognition pair
- containment / cohesion / oscillation
- dissolution / dampening / release / catalyst / abrasion / accumulation

This is much easier than memorizing all 220 forms individually.

## 7. Compact glosses are handles, not final meanings

The directional-form files use compact glosses like:
- “vitality into relation”
- “relation through vitality”
- “compression into form”
- “origin through dissolution”

These are **build-layer handles**.

They are good for:
- scanability
- quick recall
- generation support

They are not the full meaning.

When needed, a fuller derivation should still be done from:
- invariant force
- substrate definition
- directionality

Do not mistake compact glosses for final lexical translations.

## 9. Common drift risks

### 9.1 Importing English verb logic
Do not read these forms as ordinary verbs with hidden subjects and objects.

### 9.2 Over-stabilizing directional forms
A CV or VC form is not automatically a stable world.

### 9.3 Treating poetry as definition
The poetic field may help memory, but structural reading comes first.

### 9.4 Smuggling in tense
Completion, duration, and opposition may later be expressed through layering, but primitive directional forms alone should not be overread as already carrying a full tense system.

### 9.5 Treating every nice form as usable speech
Some forms are structurally valid but not yet conversationally natural.
Use should be tested, not assumed.

## 10. The operator set needs special caution

The `y`-forms in this folder are:

### CV forms
- `ya`
- `ye`
- `yi`
- `yo`
- `yu`

These describe collapse arriving into a substrate domain.

### VC forms
- `ay`
- `ey`
- `iy`
- `oy`
- `uy`

These describe a substrate domain expressing through collapse.

These forms are structurally real, but they should not be overused casually.

Why:
- `y` is operator class, not a normal kinetic
- the y-set describes becoming, unresolvedness, and collapse pressure rather than ordinary stable motion
- the CV and VC distinction still matters here, but it does not map neatly onto the same active/receptive pattern as the kinetic sets
- these forms may be especially load-bearing in names, thresholds, suspension-adjacent contexts, and later Yemu relation

Treat them as precise tools, not filler forms.

## 11. What this folder is best for right now

This folder is currently best used for:

- primitive generation
- scene experiments
- conversation experiments
- testing lexical emergence
- identifying promising stable worlds
- checking whether a derived form actually reduces cleanly to primitive logic

It is **not** yet the place to solve:
- discourse reference
- full temporal sequencing
- same-orientation governance
- advanced narrative compression

Those pressures belong to higher structural development.

The most valuable next move is not to keep expanding raw glosses.

- primitive stable worlds

## 13. Bottom line

The directional inventory is not the whole language.

It is the first generative layer where:
- primitive force
- primitive domain
- directionality
- and readable meaning

begin to meet in usable form.

Use it to generate.
Do not burden it with more theory than it needs.
