---

## 2. The main reading rule

A same-envelope primitive stable world should be read in this order:

1. identify the envelope substrate
2. identify the center
3. derive the center’s invariant function
4. derive what it means for that centered function to stabilize within the same substrate on both sides

Do not jump straight to a one-word noun gloss.

---

## 3. Why same-envelope comes first

Same-envelope worlds are the cleanest primitive stable-world baseline because:

- the outer substrate is fixed
- only the center changes
- comparison becomes easy
- the family shows what kinds of worlds one substrate can hold

This makes same-envelope worlds the best first map before:
- cross-envelope worlds
- expanded stable worlds
- non-phonetic centered balance

---

## 4. Same-envelope worlds are not “just the canonical five”

The familiar canonical worlds matter:

- `aVa`
- `iLi`
- `oRo`
- `uMu`
- `eYe`

But the point of this folder is broader.

This folder maps the full primitive same-envelope field:
- all centers
- inside each fixed substrate envelope

That lets you compare:
- which worlds feel central
- which worlds feel thin
- which may become useful in speech
- which may remain mostly structural

---

## 5. Not every same-envelope world will feel equally strong

All mapped worlds in this folder belong to the primitive same-envelope baseline.

That does not mean they will all feel:
- equally readable
- equally memorable
- equally useful in conversation
- equally likely to survive into living language

This is expected.

Some will likely become:
- major world anchors
- recurring narrative worlds
- conversational signatures

Others may remain:
- valid but rarely used
- more interpretively dense
- structurally important but socially light

---

## 6. Envelope meaning matters

The outer vowel does real work.

### `a_a`
The world stabilizes inside relation.

### `e_e`
The world stabilizes inside individuation.

### `i_i`
The world stabilizes inside appearance / field.

### `o_o`
The world stabilizes inside form.

### `u_u`
The world stabilizes inside origin.

This means the same center will feel very different across envelopes.

Examples:
- `aDa` = resisting relation
- `eDe` = resisting self
- `oDo` = resisting form
- `uDu` = resisting origin

That difference is one of the main reasons this mapping matters.

---

## 7. The y-worlds need special caution

The `y`-centered same-envelope worlds are included in the baseline because they are valid same-envelope primitive worlds.

But they should be read more cautiously than kinetic-centered worlds.

Why:
- `y` is operator class
- these worlds stabilize around collapse rather than ordinary motion
- some may feel more thresholded, crystallizing, or unstable than the others

Do not flatten `eYe` into the pattern for every `y`-world.
And do not assume all `y`-worlds are equally surface-usable.

## 8. What this folder is best for right now

This folder is currently best used for:

- mapping the primitive stable-world baseline
- identifying strong same-envelope anchors
- comparing center behavior within one envelope
- supporting conversation and story experiments
- building intuition for what kinds of worlds each substrate can hold

It is not yet the place to solve:
- cross-envelope worlds
- expanded stable-form logic
- signature phrase emergence
- CVC force-closure forms

---

## 9. What comes next

Once the same-envelope baseline is in place, the next major questions become:

- which same-envelope worlds are actually readable in use
- which become common anchors
- which invite cross-envelope comparison
- which compress into live sayings
- how cross-envelope worlds differ structurally and practically

That is why this folder comes before cross-envelope work.

---

## 10. Bottom line

Same-envelope primitive stable worlds are the cleanest first stable-world map in Varoli.

They give the language:
- a full baseline
- a learnable structure
- a comparison set
- and a stable starting point for later world expansion.
