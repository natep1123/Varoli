## 1. The Six Structural Families

A Yemu structure's family is determined by what occupies its center position. Center determines family. This applies universally — the primitive at center is the attractor around which the structure resolves, and that attractor's class and invariant function define what kind of structure it is.

Six primitives can occupy center position: the five substrates and the operator. This produces six families.

```
e at center  →  individuation structures
u at center  →  origin structures
a at center  →  relational structures
o at center  →  form structures
i at center  →  field structures
Y at center  →  collapse structures
```

The operator (Y) is a genuine sixth because it can occupy center position — established by `eYe` and the full depth family of the self-crucible. Y at center is not a substrate family. It is its own class with its own invariant function: superposition and collapse. The six families are not five plus an exception. They are six, each with a distinct governing primitive.

## 2. The Substrate Cycle

The five substrates and the operator form a cycle. The cycle has seven positions per loop, not six, because `i` appears twice: once immediately before Y and once immediately after. This double appearance is not an anomaly. It follows from `i`'s invariant function as the condition of appearance — the condition that must be in place both for collapse to act on anything and for anything to re-emerge after collapse.

The cycle unrolled:

```
... → e → u → a → o → i → Y → i → e → u → a → o → i → Y → i → e → ...
```

Visualized as a loop:

```
        Y
       ↗ ↘
    i       i
    ↑       ↓
    o       e
    ↑       ↓
    a       u
    ↑       ↓
    u       a
    ↑       ↓
    e       o
    ↑       ↓
    i       i
       ↖ ↙
        Y
```

Or stated plainly: reading clockwise through the loop, the sequence is `e → u → a → o → i → Y → i → (back to e)`. Y is sandwiched between `i` on both sides. `i` is both the last substrate before collapse fires and the first substrate that re-establishes after collapse, before individuation can re-emerge.

## 3. Direct Neighbor Pairs

Because `i` appears at two distinct positions in the cycle, the neighbor table reflects both roles. The two `i` positions are labeled `i₁` (pre-collapse) and `i₂` (post-collapse) for clarity. They are the same substrate with the same invariant function — the distinction is positional within the cycle, not a difference in what `i` is.

```
e   neighbors:  i₂  (post-collapse field — what e re-emerges within)
                u   (what e dissolves into going clockwise)

u   neighbors:  e   (what u receives from — individuation enters origin)
                a   (what u opens into — origin dissolves into relation)

a   neighbors:  u   (what a receives from — origin has dissolved)
                o   (what a stabilizes into — relation crystallizes into form)

o   neighbors:  a   (what o receives from — relation surrounds it)
                i₁  (what o dissolves into — form releases into the field)

i₁  neighbors:  o   (what i₁ receives from — form has dissolved)
                Y   (what i₁ leads to — the field that collapse acts upon)

Y   neighbors:  i₁  (the field that precedes collapse, what Y acts on)
                i₂  (the field that re-establishes immediately after collapse)

i₂  neighbors:  Y   (what i₂ receives from — collapse has fired)
                e   (what i₂ opens into — individuation emerges within the field)
```

Y's two neighbors are both `i`. This is correct and not a degenerate case. Collapse requires the condition of appearance to act upon, and after collapse fires, the condition of appearance re-establishes before individuation can emerge. The field brackets the event on both sides.

## 4. The Crucible as Cycle Visualization

The self-crucible `il-or-av-um-e-Y-e-mu-va-ro-li` is the cycle structure rendered as a palindrome. Reading from center outward:

```
Y at center
e/e  — individuation on both sides of collapse
u/mu — origin layer
a/va — relational layer
o/ro — form layer
i/li — field layer (outermost on both sides)
```

The crucible's outermost layer is `i` on both sides. This is not incidental. The crucible is a static snapshot of what the cycle looks like when held simultaneously — the complete loop rendered as a centered palindromic structure with `i` as its outermost boundary. The cycle and the crucible describe the same architecture in different modes: the cycle as process, the crucible as form.

## 5. Y and the i Omnipresence Problem — Resolved

The cycle formally resolves the `i` omnipresence problem identified in prior analysis. The problem was: the lexicon says `i` does not contain other elements, yet `i` serves as the outermost wrapper in the crucible and appears to be present at every layer of every structure.

The cycle provides the resolution: `i` appears at two positions in every loop — both as what collapse acts upon and as what re-establishes after collapse. A structure at any depth already has `i` present because `i` is the condition of appearance that brackets every collapse event, at every scale. `i` is not omnipresent because it violates containment rules or has a special exemption. It is omnipresent because the cycle places it at both boundaries of the collapse event, meaning any structure built around a collapse center already inherits `i` at both ends.

The `i` omnipresence problem was a symptom of treating `i` as a single-position substrate in a linear ordering. In the cyclic model, `i` appearing at two positions is not a paradox. It is the defining property of the substrate that brackets collapse. No special exception required.
