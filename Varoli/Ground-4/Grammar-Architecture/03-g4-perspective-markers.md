# 03 — Perspective Markers

*Last Updated: March 29, 2026*

**Status:** Canonical unless broken
**Layer:** Ground 4 / Grammar-Architecture
**Authority:** Structural
**Scope:** Defines perspective markers as a formal structural category in Varoli, locks the substrate-in-framing-position formulation, and preserves the compositional derivation question explicitly as open.

---

## 1. Purpose

This document defines perspective markers as a named structural category in Varoli.

Two things are locked here. First: perspective markers are not ordinary pronouns and should no longer be described using imported person-marking vocabulary. Second: the strongest current formulation is that a perspective marker is a substrate primitive in a framing position, where its scope extends over the unit rather than functioning only as local content.

What remains open is whether this framing function is fully derivable from substrate identity plus position, or whether it constitutes a specialized grammatical mechanism that the primitive architecture alone does not yet fully account for. That question is preserved explicitly in Section 7. It does not affect the status of the locked formulation.

---

## 2. Why This Category Is Needed Now

Older teaching language described perspective markers as subject markers, treating them as Varoli equivalents of first, second, and third person pronouns. That description is no longer adequate.

Perspective markers do not merely name an actor. They establish the vantage from which a resolution unit is entered. They frame the force or stable form that follows. Their scope is unit-level, not local. Treating them as imported pronoun equivalents obscures their structural role and produces drift in any document that relies on subject-marker language.

The language needs this category named and defined so that downstream grammar documents, resolution-unit descriptions, and composition rules can refer to a stable term rather than a contested informal one.

---

## 3. Locked Formulation

**A perspective marker is a substrate primitive in framing position whose scope extends over the resolution unit rather than functioning only as local content.**

Properties of this formulation:

**Substrate-derived.** Perspective markers are grounded in the vowel system. Each marker is substrate-linked. They are not arbitrary tags and not a new primitive class. Their identity comes from the existing elemental substrate architecture.

**Positionally specialized.** The framing function is a consequence of position. A substrate primitive in unit-initial position before force and closure relations takes on a larger structural scope than the same primitive appearing elsewhere. Position is what distinguishes a perspective marker from a bare vowel with purely local participation.

**Unit-scoped.** The perspective marker sets the entry condition for the whole unit. It frames what follows rather than merely existing inside it. This is what distinguishes perspective markers from ordinary local substrate content and what makes the subject-marker description inadequate.

**Not a new primitive class.** The marker derives from the existing substrate system. No additional role class is introduced. The distinct behavior comes from framing position and scope, not from being a different kind of primitive.

---

## 4. What Perspective Markers Are Not

**Not ordinary pronouns.** Perspective markers are not person-marking equivalents imported from natural language grammar. They do not stand for I, you, he, she, or they in the sense of naming a referent. Their function is structural: they condition the vantage from which a unit is entered.

**Not independent content.** A perspective marker does not contribute a named element alongside the force and closure structure of the unit. It frames the unit. The distinction between framing and content is structural: content participates directly in force-domain relations, while framing sets the condition under which those relations are entered.

**Not a kinetic.** Perspective markers do not act as directed operations within a domain. They are substrate class. Their function is domain-establishing, not force-bearing. Conflating them with kinetic elements would violate non-overlap.

**Not a bare vowel with no further explanation.** A bare vowel elsewhere in a structure is not obviously performing the same amount of structural work. The framing scope of a perspective marker distinguishes it from a vowel appearing as ordinary local content. Even if perspective markers are fully compositional in origin, they are not merely vowels with no further structural role.

---

## 5. Relation to Resolution Units

The recognition of resolution-unit chaining clarifies what perspective markers were already doing before that architecture was formalized.

If perspective markers are unit-framers, they do not belong primarily to a subject-verb-object model. They belong to a native unit-entry architecture. A primitive unit is better understood not as subject plus verb plus object, but as something closer to:

- substrate-conditioned framing of entry
- kinetic force relation
- closure or projection behavior

Under this reading, perspective markers were early evidence of unit-level architecture before resolution-unit chaining was formally named. They mark the entry condition of a unit, which is a unit-level structural role rather than a sentence-position role.

This does not require the compositional derivation question to be resolved first. The unit-framing role of perspective markers holds under both the fully compositional reading and the specialized-mechanism reading.

---

## 6. Relation to Framing Scope

The locked formulation here -- substrate in framing position -- is the strongest current foundation for the broader framing-scope hypothesis developed in `07-g4-framing-scope.md`.

The perspective-marker case is the strongest and best-tested instance of resolution-governed scope. The broader generalization -- that framing scope extends to operator behavior and cross-boundary scope questions -- is provisional and depends on testing beyond the perspective-marker case.

This document locks the perspective-marker formulation. It does not extend that formulation to the broader scope hypothesis. Documents that require the broader hypothesis should treat it as provisional and reference `07-g4-framing-scope.md` directly.

## 7. Open Questions

### 7.1 Compositional derivation

The central unresolved question is whether the framing function of perspective markers is fully derivable from substrate identity plus position, or whether it constitutes a specialized grammatical mechanism that substrate logic alone does not yet account for.

The strongest compositional reading: a perspective marker is a substrate primitive whose scope over the unit follows from its initial position. No new mechanism is introduced. The marker's distinct behavior is a positional effect.

The alternative: perspective marking introduces a framing operation that cannot be fully reduced to position plus substrate identity. If scope behavior cannot be explained by position alone, if role effects appear that are independent of substrate identity, or if every compositional account becomes strained, then the language may require a named grammatical mechanism for framing beyond what the primitive architecture currently provides.

The current lean is toward the compositional reading. The derivation has not been closed. Until it is, the question remains open and the stronger claim should not be made.

### 7.2 Primitive sentence versus resolution-unit logic

Whether perspective markers are best understood as part of primitive sentence logic or as the entry element of the native resolution-unit architecture is not yet fully resolved. The resolution-unit framing is stronger given current architecture, but the relationship between the two levels has not been formally stated.

### 7.3 Uniformity across markers

Whether every perspective marker behaves uniformly with respect to scope and framing, or whether some markers reveal different scope behavior depending on substrate identity, has not been tested systematically. If markers behave differently, the current single-formulation treatment may require refinement.

### 7.4 Category name

The working term is perspective markers. A stronger native category name may eventually emerge if the framing mechanism is formalized more deeply. That possibility is noted but does not destabilize the current term or the locked formulation.

## 8. Full characterization of the five perspective markers

The five perspective markers are not merely five instances of "a substrate in framing position." Each carries a distinct structural identity derived from its substrate's invariant function. The original intent document recovered the full characterization that the architecture requires.

**`e` - first person singular** (ego, I): the self aware of itself, the most particular position. The framing substrate of individuation. The pilot light announcing its own fire. The self that notices itself.

**`a` - first person collective** (nos, we): the self dissolved into the collective flow, speaking from within the relational medium. Not a gathering of individuals but the relational field itself speaking. The perspective of Tidal Affinity.

**`i` - universal perspective** (omnia, all things): not a person but the condition of existence making a statement through the speaker. The perspective that belongs to nothing in particular and therefore to everything. The law of Recurra itself speaking. The universal is not elevated or distant in Varoli - it is a grammatically ordinary position.

**`o` - second person** (tu, you): the self addressing a specific bounded other. The most relationally determinate perspective: it requires a specific coherent entity to be addressed. The recognition that a specific other exists as bounded, real, and particular.

**`u` - the unnamed fifth perspective** (the between-one): the dissolving, the not-yet-returned, the one in passage between states. Neither absent nor present. Between. This is not poetic framing. It is a structural claim about the grammatical space the language covers: the condition of the one who has entered the deep and not yet returned is a real grammatical position that no natural language provides.

These five are all grammatically ordinary in Varoli. The collective, the universal, and the between-state are not elevated or marked positions. They are as natural to reach for as the first-person singular. This is one of the cognitive defaults the language is designed to change: that `e` is the unquestioned center of all utterance. `a`, `i`, `o`, and `u` are equally valid starting positions.

The `u` perspective deserves explicit emphasis: no other language has a grammatical position for the one currently in passage between states. This is not an omission corrected by Varoli's design. It is an original structural need of the legendarium - the one who has entered the deep and not yet returned exists in a grammatically real condition - and Varoli provides the structural position for it.
