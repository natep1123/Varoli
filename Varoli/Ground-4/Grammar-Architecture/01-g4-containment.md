# 02 — Containment

*Last Updated: March 27, 2026*

**Status:** Canonical unless broken
**Layer:** Ground 4 / Grammar-Architecture
**Authority:** Structural
**Scope:** Defines containment as a formal native structural relation in Varoli.

---

## 1. Purpose

This document defines containment as a named structural relation in Varoli.

Containment is already operative throughout the language. It governs how kinetic forces relate to substrate domains, how stable forms close, how deep reception works, and how multiple forces coexist within a shared domain. It has never been formally defined as a named category with stated properties and explicit boundaries.

This document corrects that.

---

## 2. Why This Category Is Needed Now

Containment is invoked in every major structural operation Varoli performs beyond primitive composition:

- When a directional force resolves within a stable form, the mechanism is kinetic within substrate. That is containment.
- When a resolution unit closes, the stable form absorbs the preceding kinetic force through the containment relation. That is containment governing closure.
- When projected force crosses a unit boundary and resolves within a later stable domain, the relation between that force and its receiving domain is the same kinetic-within-substrate relation. That is containment governing deep reception.
- When multiple forces resolve within a shared domain, the structural ground they share is the domain that contains them both. That is containment governing multi-force holding.

Every one of these operations currently describes containment without defining it. The phrase "the containment relationship: kinetic within substrate" appears across documents as a shared assumption rather than a defined structural category. The language cannot scale further without making this explicit.

---

## 3. Definition

**Containment** is the structural relation in which a kinetic force operates within a substrate domain without altering the domain's identity.

---

## 4. Properties

### 4.1 Class-governed

Containment is a relation between primitives of different role classes: kinetic class within substrate class. It is not a relation between two substrates, two kinetics, or any same-class pairing. This follows directly from non-overlap (ICA Axiom 1): role classes are absolutely separated, and containment describes specifically how kinetic operation occurs within the condition established by substrate. No other class pairing produces containment as defined here.

### 4.2 Non-destructive

The contained force does not alter the substrate. The domain remains what it is. The force acts within it. This is not a claim about the force being passive. It is a claim about the domain's invariance under containment. The substrate's function does not change because a kinetic force is operating within it.

### 4.3 Provenance-blind

Containment does not distinguish forces by compositional origin. A force composed within the current resolution unit and a force that arrived via projection from a prior unit are both contained by the same mechanism. No primitive, rule, or role class encodes origin. This is the working hypothesis that grounds deep reception as a general structural expectation rather than a case-by-case judgment. If a construction forces origin-sensitivity, this property must be revisited.

### 4.4 Structurally readable

A contained force is identifiable within its domain. Its invariant function is preserved. The domain's substrate identity is preserved. The composition of force-within-domain produces a readable structural meaning: what this force does within this condition. This readability is what makes stable forms interpretable from center outward.

### 4.5 Finite but not yet bounded capacity

A domain can contain more than one force. Two-force containment is tested and supported. Three-force containment has been encountered but is not yet directly validated. Whether a simple VCV stable form can contain arbitrarily many forces is unknown. Larger stable forms may be required for higher force loads. The upper bound, if one exists, is not yet derived.

---

## 5. What Containment Explains

### 5.1 Stable form closure

A resolution unit closes when its terminal element is a stable form. The mechanism: the stable form's domain contains the preceding kinetic force. Closure is containment completing locally.

### 5.2 Center-outward reading of stable forms

The attractor at center is the identity of the form. The surrounding domain is the substrate condition. The forces within are contained by the domain. Reading from center outward is reading the attractor, then the containment relation, then the domain boundary.

### 5.3 Deep reception

Projected force from a prior unit resolves within a later unit's stable domain. The mechanism: containment governs reception because the relation between kinetic and substrate operates on class membership, not on compositional history. Provenance-blindness (Property 4.3) is what makes deep reception structurally continuous with local containment rather than a separate mechanism.

### 5.4 Multi-force coexistence

When two or more forces resolve within the same stable domain, they are both contained by the same substrate. Their co-presence is structurally possible because containment does not limit a domain to one force. Their inter-force relation (governed by orientation) is a question about how contained forces relate to each other within the domain, not about whether the domain can hold them.

### 5.5 The `arOva` precedent

The stable form `arOva` contains two kinetic forces (`r` and `v`) within one substrate domain (`a`). The capital `O` marks the attractor at structural center. Both forces are contained. Both are readable. The stable form is coherent. This is the strongest current confirmation of within-form multi-force containment, verified through centered closure, with strict phonemic palindrome as its clearest current manifestation.

## 6. What Containment Is Not

**Not predication.** Predication assigns a property to a subject. Containment does not assign properties. The force does not describe the domain. It acts within it.

**Not possession.** Possession indicates ownership or belonging. The domain does not own the force. The force does not own the domain. They stand in a structural relation determined by class membership.

**Not syntactic government.** Government is a relation between syntactic heads and dependents in tree-based models. Containment is not tree-based. It is not about dominance or hierarchical dependency. It is about the structural condition of kinetic operation within substrate condition.

**Not identity.** A force contained within a domain does not become the domain or merge with it. Both retain their class membership and their invariant functions. Containment preserves distinction.

**Not operator-in-substrate.** When an operator class primitive occupies a position within a substrate domain (as in `eYe`), that relation is not covered by this definition. Containment as defined here is strictly kinetic-within-substrate. Whether operator-in-substrate is the same relation, a variant, or a structurally distinct category is an open question treated in Section 7.

## 7. Open Questions

### 7.1 Capacity bounds

Does a simple VCV stable form have a containment limit? If so, is the limit derivable from the architecture or determined empirically? Does exceeding the limit require larger stable forms, nested structures, or does it produce structural incoherence? This is not yet resolved.

### 7.2 Cross-boundary verification

Within-form containment is verifiable through palindromic closure and centered balance. Cross-boundary containment (deep reception) is not verifiable through the same mechanism. Whether there is a native verification method for cross-boundary containment, or whether provenance-blindness functions as a sufficient architectural guarantee, is not yet determined.

### 7.3 Operator-in-substrate

When `y` appears at center of a stable form (as in `eYe`), the operator is in attractor position within a substrate domain. The operator is not a kinetic. Containment as defined here does not cover this case. Whether operator-in-substrate is the same structural relation, a variant of it, or a distinct category requiring its own treatment is explicitly open and should not be resolved by extending the current definition prematurely.

### 7.4 Containment as a derived law

Containment is implicit in the role class definitions: substrate establishes condition, kinetic operates within it. Whether containment should be stated as an explicit derived law in `../../../Ground-0/01-g0-ica-blueprint.md`, or whether it remains fully derivable from existing laws without requiring its own entry, is a question for Ground 0 review. It is flagged here but not resolved here.
