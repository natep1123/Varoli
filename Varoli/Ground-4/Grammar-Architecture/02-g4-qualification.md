# 02 — Qualification

*Last Updated: March 28, 2026*

**Status:** Canonical unless broken
**Layer:** Ground 4 / Grammar-Architecture
**Authority:** Structural
**Scope:** Defines qualification as a formal native structural category in Varoli, covering three attested instances and stating the open unity question explicitly.

---

## 1. Purpose

This document defines qualification as a named structural category in Varoli.

Qualification is already operative throughout the language in at least three distinct structural contexts. Each context has been described locally using its own vocabulary. No document has named the shared structural pattern, stated its properties, or asked whether the three instances constitute one principle or a family of related mechanisms.

This document names the category, defines it, records the three attested instances, states what qualification explains, states what it is not, and preserves the unity question explicitly as unresolved.

---

## 2. Why This Category Is Needed Now

The language currently performs specification in at least three ways:

- Consonant suffixes qualify verb force through verb layering.
- Surrounding vowels in a palindromic stable form specify the domain that holds the attractor.
- Outer layers in centered expansion contextualize the attractor without replacing it.

Each of these is described in its own context with its own local vocabulary. But all three share a structural pattern: one element adds specification to another element's contribution without replacing it, without merging class identities, and without altering the qualified element's invariant function.

If this is one structural principle, it should be named. If it is a family of related but structurally distinct mechanisms, the distinctions should be stated. Either result advances the language. What cannot continue is the current state where specification happens through unnamed, unrelated local descriptions across three separate grammar areas.

---

## 3. Definition

**Qualification** is the structural relation in which one primitive contributes its invariant function as a specification of another primitive's structural role, without replacing the qualified element, without merging class identities, and without altering the qualified element's own invariant function.

The qualifier adds specification. The qualified element retains identity. The composition of the two is more structurally resolved than either alone.

---

## 4. Attested Instances

### 4.1 Suffix qualification

A consonant following a CV verb contributes its invariant kinetic function as a specification of the verb's force. The verb's force is qualified, not replaced. The suffix does not establish its own independent force-domain relationship. The result is a compound force: the original mode, narrowed by the suffix's kinetic function.

Structure: `CV + C` (e.g., `vek`). The suffix `k` qualifies the force `ve`. The result is individuation-with-completion, not a new verb and not two independent verbs.

Suffix order is meaningful because each qualifier operates on the already-qualified result of everything to its left. `vekp` and `vepk` are structurally distinct because each suffix qualifies a different input. This follows directly from qualification as a structural relation: the qualifier specifies what is already there.

### 4.2 Domain qualification

In a VCV stable form, the surrounding vowel specifies the domain within which the attractor is held. The vowel does not change the attractor's function. It provides the substrate condition of containment. The attractor retains its identity. The domain adds specification: this force is held within this condition.

Structure: `VCV` (e.g., `aVa`). The surrounding `a` qualifies the contained attractor by specifying its domain. The attractor retains its function. The composition is force-held-within-condition, not force alone and not condition alone.

Domain qualification depends on the containment relation (defined in `01-g4-containment.md`): the domain vowel qualifies the attractor by specifying the substrate condition of its containment. Qualification and containment are not the same relation. Containment is the structural relation by which a kinetic force operates within a substrate domain. Qualification is the relation by which one element adds specification to another's structural role. In domain qualification, both relations are simultaneously operative: the domain contains the force and qualifies it by specifying the condition of that containment.

### 4.3 Contextual qualification

In a larger stable form, outer layers add structural resolution around the attractor without displacing it. Each additional layer qualifies the center by providing contextual specification. The center retains identity. The layers add resolution.

Structure: `V1-C1-C2-C1-V1` (e.g., `arOva`). The outer layers (`a`, `r`, `v`) qualify the attractor `O` by contextualizing it. The attractor `O` retains its function. The capital `O` marks the attractor at structural center. The composition preserves the attractor's identity while increasing its structural resolution.

## 5. What Qualification Explains

### 5.1 Why verb layering does not change terminal behavior

If the suffix is a qualifier rather than an independent structural element, it does not introduce a new force-domain relationship. It narrows the existing one. The terminal element is still the qualified force, not a new structural unit. The force still projects because there is still no stable domain to contain it. Terminal behavior is governed by the force's structural position, not by its internal qualification.

### 5.2 Why centered expansion preserves attractor identity

If outer layers are qualifiers, they do not replace the center. They specify it. The attractor remains the identity-bearing element because qualification adds to it rather than substituting for it. This is why stable forms are read from center outward: the attractor is the identity, and the surrounding structure is its qualification.

### 5.3 Why domain vowels in stable forms are not independent content

The surrounding vowel in a VCV form is not a second named element alongside the attractor. It is the qualification that specifies the condition within which the attractor is held. This is consistent with center-outward reading: the attractor is primary, the domain specification is its qualification.

### 5.4 Why suffix order matters

Each suffix qualifies the result of everything to its left. Ordering is meaningful because each qualifier operates on an already-qualified input. This follows directly from the definition: qualification adds specification to a structural role that already exists. The input to each successive qualifier is the compound result of all prior qualification.

---

## 6. What Qualification Is Not

**Not modification in the English grammatical sense.** English modification is a broad syntactic category defined by position-based or dependency-based rules. Qualification in Varoli is class-based and compositional: a primitive of a given class adds its invariant function to the structural role of an adjacent element, governed by class membership and position, not by syntactic category membership.

**Not predication.** Predication assigns a property or state to a subject. Qualification does not assign. It specifies. The qualified element is not described by the qualifier. The qualifier adds structural resolution to the qualified element's own contribution.

**Not a fourth role class.** Qualification is not performed by a new type of primitive. It is performed by existing primitives (kinetics and substrates) in specific positional relationships. The qualifier is always a primitive whose role class is already defined. Its role as qualifier is a function of its position relative to the qualified element, not a new class membership.

**Not containment.** Containment is the relation by which a kinetic force operates within a substrate domain without altering the domain's identity. Qualification is the relation by which one primitive's invariant function adds specification to another's structural role. In domain qualification, both relations are simultaneously operative, but they are distinct. A qualified force is not thereby contained. A contained force is not thereby qualified except in the specific case of domain qualification, where both hold at once.

## 7. Open Questions

### 7.1 Unity question

The three attested instances share the pattern: one element adds specification to another's structural role without replacing the qualified element's identity, merging class membership, or altering the qualified element's invariant function. Whether this constitutes a single structural principle or a family of related but structurally distinct mechanisms is explicitly unresolved.

The qualification category itself is strong enough to lock. The three instances are real. The language needs the category now. What remains open is narrower: whether a single formal statement can predict the behavior of all three instances, including what they permit and what they prohibit. If it can, qualification is one principle. If different instances require different formal rules, they are related but structurally distinct mechanisms that share a common label for practical purposes.

The strongest test for unity: construct a single formal statement of the qualification principle and verify whether it correctly predicts (a) that suffix order matters in verb layering, (b) that domain vowels in stable forms do not override the attractor, and (c) that outer layers in centered expansion contextualize without displacing. If all three follow from the same statement without additional rules, qualification is one principle.

### 7.2 Substrate-to-kinetic qualification

All attested qualification flows in specific directions: kinetic qualifies kinetic (suffix layering), substrate qualifies attractor-in-domain (stable form structure), substrate-and-kinetic qualifies attractor (centered expansion). Whether a bare substrate can qualify a kinetic directly, outside the stable-form containment context, has not been tested. If it can, the qualification category is broader than currently attested. If it cannot, qualification may be constrained by the class of the qualified element in ways not yet formalized.

### 7.3 Operator qualification

Whether `y` can qualify a force in the way a consonant suffix does is not yet determined. If `y` can qualify, it does so as an operator, not as a kinetic. That would mean qualification is not restricted to kinetic-to-kinetic or substrate-to-attractor relationships. The implications for operator scope and for the open question of `y` as suffix would be significant. This should not be assumed before testing.

### 7.4 Containment and qualification as orthogonal relations

When a qualified (layered) force resolves within a stable domain, the containment relation holds the compound force. Whether containment is aware of the internal qualification of the force it contains, or whether it treats the compound force as atomic, is not yet formally resolved. The current architecture treats compound forces as atomic during projection and reception. If this holds, containment is qualification-blind: it contains whatever force arrives regardless of internal structure. That would make containment and qualification orthogonal structural relations, which is a clean result, but it has not been verified explicitly.
