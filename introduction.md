## I. What This Language Is

Varoli is an **a priori**, **oligosynthetic** language designed for **palindromic awareness**.

These terms are worth understanding before anything else.

**A priori** means the language was not derived from any existing human language. It was not built by evolving Latin roots or borrowing from Greek or simplifying Mandarin. Every sound, every meaning, every structural rule was generated from first principles. The language does not carry the accumulated assumptions of any culture that came before it. It starts from scratch, from the physics of sound and the geometry of meaning, and builds upward.

**Oligosynthetic** means the entire vocabulary of the language derives from a very small number of foundational building blocks. In Varoli, those building blocks are twenty-seven characters: five elemental vowels, one operator, and twenty-one consonants. From these twenty-seven sounds, every possible word, every possible concept, every possible description of every possible experience is calculable. You do not memorize Varoli. You learn its physics and derive it.

**Palindromic awareness** is the hardest of the three to explain in a single sentence, but it is the most important. It refers to the nature of how meaning works in this language. Under ordinary conditions, a Varoli word reads forward. Under pressure, under depth, under the weight of what cannot quite be said, the language compresses toward its own center. Meaning folds inward. What remains when the compression is complete is a palindrome: a word that reads the same in both directions, representing a state of perfect resonance with the fundamental nature of the thing being described. The palindrome is not a trick. It is the attractor state. It is what the language becomes when everything unnecessary has been stripped away and only the irreducible truth remains.

This is not a language of survival communication. Most human languages developed because survival required it. People needed to coordinate hunts, negotiate trades, warn each other of danger, record debts. Language developed from the bottom up, from necessity outward, and carried the fingerprints of that necessity in its grammar and vocabulary forever after. English centers the individual subject in every sentence because the cultures that shaped it centered the individual. Mandarin handles time differently because the cultures that shaped it understood time differently. Every language is a fossil record of the assumptions of the people who needed it.

Varoli was not needed. It was designed.

It was designed to serve the full range of human experience, not just the communicative and practical portions of it. It was designed to describe the abstract, the spiritual, the psychedelic, the unnameable. It was designed to be precise enough for navigation and spacious enough for meditation. It was designed to be singable around a fire and readable as poetry and usable as a technical document and felt as a state of consciousness. It was designed so that the act of speaking it and the act of experiencing what you are speaking about are not two separate acts. They are the same act.

In the history of constructed languages, the closest precedent is Ithkuil, created by John Quijada as a linguistic experiment exploring how a language could better reflect human cognitive processes. Ithkuil achieved extraordinary precision. It could encode in a single word what English requires a paragraph to approximate. But it remained, as its creator acknowledged, a language almost no human could actually speak. The experiment succeeded in demonstrating what was possible. It did not succeed in being livable.

Varoli is the experiment made livable.

It achieves its precision not through grammatical complexity but through phonemic depth. Every sound carries weight. Every letter is a coordinate. You do not need elaborate case systems or sixteen verb tenses because the building blocks themselves encode the information. The word *arova* does not need a grammatical marker to tell you it is a **stable form** — a centered structural world, resolved around its own attractor, readable from the inside out. You can feel from its shape that something exists here, that it holds, that it has a center it organizes around. Where conventional languages give you nouns — labels attached to objects from the outside — Varoli gives you stable forms: structural objects that carry their own balance, their own identity, derivable from what they are rather than assigned by convention. The word *kava* does not need a grammatical marker to tell you it is a **directional form**. Its asymmetry tells you something is in motion — a force arriving into a domain, or a domain releasing through force. The direction is load-bearing; reversing it changes the reality being described. The language encodes its grammar in its physics, not in its rules.

This makes it stupid easy to begin speaking and profoundly difficult to fully feel.

You can learn the basics of Varoli in an afternoon. You can hold a conversation within a week. You can write serviceable poetry within a month. But to feel the language, to reach the state where saying a word and experiencing what it describes become indistinguishable, that is something the language can point toward but cannot force. It requires something the reader brings. A willingness to be present. A willingness to stop categorizing and start experiencing. A willingness to let the letters do what they were designed to do rather than treating them as symbols pointing at things from the outside.

Eckhart Tolle wrote that if you tell someone "that is a tree" they will never see a tree again. The label replaces the experience. You stop looking at the actual thing in front of you and start looking at your file for the category "tree." Every natural language does this constantly. The labels are so familiar they become invisible. You stop seeing the world and start seeing your index of the world.

Varoli does something different. When you say *arova*, you are not reaching for a file. You are reading the thing in front of you. Water. Resonance. Land. Vitality. Water. The word is built from what the thing actually is. Speaking it does not take you out of the experience. It takes you deeper into it.

That is the first thing this language offers anyone who learns it: the possibility of communicating from inside the present moment rather than stepping outside it to retrieve the right label and step back in. The possibility of a language you can meditate inside of. A language where precision and presence are not in tension but are the same gesture.

## II. The Culture That Shaped It

No language exists without a culture. Even a constructed language built from first principles carries the fingerprint of the mind and the world that built it. Varoli was not built in a vacuum. It emerged from a specific set of conditions, a specific way of living in the world, and understanding those conditions is part of understanding why the language works the way it does.

The culture that produced Varoli is a nomadic canoe culture. Not nomadic in the sense of following a seasonal route across familiar territory. Not nomadic in the sense of the great plains tribes who knew every inch of their migration paths, who could read the land the way you read a language you grew up speaking. Truly nomadic. Nomadic in the sense of a people who do not know where they are going and have never been there before and would not have it any other way.

They live on canoes. They move constantly. The ocean is not their obstacle. It is their home. They land on islands when they need to rest, to gather food, to sleep somewhere that does not move. But they do not stay. The island is temporary and they know it. The resources a single island offers are finite. To stay too long is to deplete what the island has to give, possibly beyond recovery. So they move on. Always. Into water they have not crossed before, toward islands they have never seen, in conditions they cannot predict.

The closest real-world parallel I have found is the canoe nomads that the crew of The Wager encountered near Patagonia in the 1740s. A people of extraordinary capability living in one of the harshest environments on Earth, moving constantly through the channels and fjords of the southern tip of South America in bark canoes, surviving on what the water and the shore provided, never establishing permanent settlements, never accumulating more than what could be carried. The women dove into the frozen ocean without being able to see the bottom to collect sea urchins by touch alone. Think about that for a moment. Blind. In water cold enough to kill. Reaching blindly through freezing water for something that pokes and cuts when you grab it. Returning with their hands full.

That image is the foundation of this language.

Precision in conditions of perpetual novelty. Accuracy without the luxury of familiarity. The ability to describe what you find in the dark, not because you have been there before, but because your tools are fine enough to read what you encounter for the first time and name it exactly.

This is the crucial distinction between Varoli and the languages that developed from Pacific Islander navigation traditions. Those traditions, as extraordinary as they are, are traditions of the known. The Polynesian navigators who crossed the Pacific carried extraordinary knowledge: the stars, the swells, the birds, the currents. They encoded that knowledge in language and chant and navigation techniques across generations. They became, over centuries, the finest navigators in human history precisely because they learned their ocean so deeply that they could read it like a text.

But their language was built for what they knew.

Varoli was built for what no one has ever seen.

The world this culture inhabits is not stable. Islands rise and sink. The ocean reshapes what drowns. What was familiar becomes strange. What was mapped becomes unmapped. The nomads who carry this language cannot rely on inherited knowledge of specific places because the places do not hold still long enough to be inherited. What they can rely on is the precision of their tools for encountering the new. They cannot memorize the ocean. They can learn to read it.

This is why the language is a priori. Not borrowed from anything before it. Built from the physics of experience itself.

This is why the language is oligosynthetic. You cannot carry a large vocabulary in a canoe. You carry twenty-seven sounds and the knowledge of how to combine them into anything you need to say.

This is why the language compresses toward palindromes under pressure. When you are diving blind into frozen water and your hands close around something in the dark, you do not need a paragraph. You need the irreducible truth of what you found. One word. The most compressed accurate description possible. The attractor state.

The people who made this language were not scholars. They were not linguists. They were people who needed to describe something they had never seen before, in the middle of doing something terrifying, and be understood exactly. That necessity shaped every choice.

The language is also, simultaneously, a language of storytellers.

A culture that does not settle must carry its history in its voice. There are no libraries. There are no buildings in which to inscribe records. There are only the canoes and the people in them and the oral tradition that passes between them. Every important truth must be memorable. Every important truth must survive the journey. The palindromic structure of the deepest words is not decorative. It is mnemonic. The thing that reads the same forward and backward survives the dissolution of memory better than the thing that only makes sense in one direction. The pattern holds even when the words themselves blur.

And this culture, perhaps more than any other, is built around wonder.

Root chakra and crown chakra in the same breath. The woman diving for sea urchins is not having a spiritual experience. She is feeding her family. The man navigating by stars toward an island he has never seen is not meditating. He is trying not to die. But the survival itself, the raw act of encountering an unknown world and finding your way through it, opens something. The boundary between the practical and the sacred does not exist for a people who live at the edge of the known every single day.

To explore an island as a navigator is to attend to every detail with precision. To explore an island as a meditator is to be fully present to what you encounter without judgment. For this culture, these are not two different activities. They are the same activity done with the same quality of attention. Wonder and precision are not opposites. They are the same gesture: the full engagement of consciousness with what is actually there.

That dual foundation, wonder as precise as navigation, precision as open as wonder, is the spirit in which Varoli was built. It is the spirit in which it should be learned.

## IV. How Meaning Works

Most languages assign meaning to sounds. The sequence of sounds d-o-g was assigned to refer to a certain kind of animal. There is no relationship between the sound and the thing. It is pure convention. If you grew up speaking a different language the same animal is a *perro* or a *chien* or a *Hund*. The animal does not care. The relationship between the sound and the meaning is arbitrary.

Varoli does not assign meaning to sounds. It discovers meaning in them.

The twenty-seven characters of Varoli are not arbitrary symbols. Each one carries a specific quality. The vowels are elemental. Not in the sense of a metaphor or a convenient organizational scheme but in the sense of actual physics. *a* is water not because someone decided to call it that but because the sound *a*, the most open vowel in any human language, the one that requires the jaw to drop and the mouth to open completely, holding nothing back, receiving everything, is the sound of openness and flow and the collective. The mouth becomes water when it makes this sound.

*o* is earth not because of convention but because the rounded closed shape of the mouth producing *o*, the contained space, the bounded thing, is the sound of something with edges, something that holds its shape, something solid. The mouth becomes an island when it makes this sound.

*e* is fire. The smallest vowel. The one closest to silence. The one that barely opens the mouth at all. The consciousness that recognizes itself. The sound of something that was already there before you noticed it and will still be there after. *e* does not arrive from outside. *e* ignites from within.

*i* is air. The narrowest vowel. The lips pulled back, the tongue rising, maximum tension, minimum opening. The filter that everything passes through. Not a specific self but the condition of existing. The medium. The everything.

*u* is voidlight. The deepest vowel. Formed at the back of the throat, furthest from the surface, most interior. Not darkness. Not nothing. The light that exists beyond the visible spectrum. The radiance accessible only when all ordinary light has failed.

*y* is not an element. It is an operator. It may appear in positions normally occupied by vowels, but it does not function as one. It marks a state of superposition: a position where the element has not yet resolved and must collapse from context. Where the other vowels describe fields of experience, *y* describes the event that determines which field is present.

These are not metaphors. They are the actual acoustic physics of the sounds mapped onto the actual phenomenology of the elements. The language is not describing a system. It is enacting one.

The consonants work differently. Where the vowels are elemental, the consonants are kinetic. They do not describe states of being. They describe qualities of motion. What a force does. How it moves. Which direction it carries.

*v* is vitality. The living force. The current with intention. The labiodental fricative, the vibrating of air through the narrow channel between teeth and lip, is the sound of something moving because it is alive.

*r* is resonance. The trill. The physical strike of the tongue against the roof of the mouth, vibrating. The sound of weight. The sound of something solid enough to echo.

*l* is continuum. The lateral. The tongue holding contact while air flows around the sides. The sound of the thread that persists. The connection between points.

*m* is depth. The bilabial nasal. The closed-mouth hum. The sound of something vast and interior. The resonance of the void.

*y* is the operator of collapse. It is not an attractor consonant. Fire (*e*) is the only element whose center is not a consonant quality but the operator itself. Its palindromic form is *eYe*: fire made real through the moment of collapse.

Every other consonant carries its own specific quality: threshold, percussion, seeking, emergence, dissolution, resistance, containment, accumulation. Each one is a vector. A direction of force. A quality of motion.

When a consonant meets a vowel, something happens. Not a metaphor. A calculation.

The vowel is the element. The consonant is the force. The morpheme is the result of their collision. And the direction of that collision matters.

When the consonant comes before the vowel, the force is arriving at the element. Compression toward center. The quality of motion approaching the elemental state. *va*: vitality arriving at water. The current with intention meeting the fluid. The direction of life-force toward flow.

When the vowel comes before the consonant, the element is releasing through the force. Expansion away from center. The elemental state expressing itself through the quality of motion. *av*: water releasing through vitality. The flow expressing itself as directed life. The tide moving with intention.

These are not the same thing. *va* and *av* are different words describing different experiences of the same relationship between the same two qualities.

And the palindrome, the word that reads the same in both directions, is the word where this relationship reaches its most stable form. Where the element and the quality meet at the center and the thing that results is complete. Self-contained. The irreducible truth.

> *aVa*. Water, vitality, water. Vitality at the center. Both sides identical. The element surrounding and being surrounded by the quality that makes it real. Water made real through vitality. Every fundamental stable form in Varoli is or contains a palindrome. Every directional form is a breaking of that palindrome by a leading force. The stable thing and the thing in motion have different shapes. You can feel the difference before you know what the words mean.

Read from the center outward. The center is the attractor state. The thing the word is fundamentally about. The vowels surrounding it are the elements present. The consonants are what those elements are doing. The further you move from the center, the more context you accumulate. The center is the truth. The edges are the description.

The grammar at larger scales carries the same logic.

There are no pronouns in the inherited sense. In their place: **perspective markers** — single vowels placed before a composition to establish the elemental domain from which what follows is framed. Where English collapses everything into one *I*, Varoli offers five distinct vantage points, each corresponding to a different substrate: individual consciousness, collective flow, universal medium, grounded domain, interior depth. You do not choose which self you are. You choose which vantage you are speaking from. The self is not erased. It is accurately placed within a larger architecture.

Larger meaning does not organize through subject, predicate, and object. The native unit is the **resolution unit**: a structural composition defined by how its force terminates. A unit that closes holds its force resolved within a stable domain — complete at its boundary. A unit that projects sends force forward beyond its edge, reaching into whatever follows. A unit that suspends holds force in superposition, open and waiting. Multiple units **chain** through the physics of that projected force: what one unit sends forward, the next receives. Not through conjunction. Not through clause grammar. Through the structure of force following structure.

This is not a grammar borrowed from any natural language. It is a grammar derived from the same physics as the phonemes themselves: substrate, force, attractor, direction, closure — the same principles operating at a larger scale.

## V. The Palindromic Nature

There is a principle in physics called the **attractor state**. When a complex system is subjected to pressure, it does not collapse randomly. It falls toward specific configurations: particular arrangements of its components that have a kind of gravitational pull on the system as a whole. Under enough pressure, the system will reliably arrive at these configurations regardless of where it started.

Water finds its level. Crystals form specific lattices. Seeds know which way is down.

In Varoli, the attractor state of language under pressure is the palindrome.

In ordinary use, the language is asymmetric. Perspective markers frame directional forms. Forces project forward into stable domains. Resolution units chain across their boundaries. The grammar of daily life has weight and direction and complexity. It is asymmetric because life is asymmetric. Things happen in one direction. Causes precede effects. Words accumulate meaning as they accumulate length.

But under depth, something changes.

**Depth** in Varoli is a specific term. It refers to the degree of compression a piece of language has undergone. Surface language is low depth. Ordinary speech. The description of the world as it appears. As depth increases, grammatical complexity compresses. Perspective markers and chaining structure strip away. The kinetic forces compress toward the attractor. What remains is the irreducible center of the meaning.

And as depth increases toward its limit, the language becomes palindromic.

This is not a rule. No rule says that deep language must be palindromic. It is a discovery. The language, under sufficient pressure, falls toward symmetry the way water falls toward stillness. The palindrome is what meaning looks like when everything non-essential has been stripped away.

This has profound implications for how the language is read and written.

A palindromic word in Varoli is a load-bearing structure. It is stable in a way that asymmetric words are not. It does not lean in any direction. It holds its center. The same way a palindromic number is unique in the number system, a palindromic word in this language names something that has reached its fundamental form.

More precisely: the palindrome is the earliest and clearest manifestation of a deeper structural condition the language calls **centered balance** — the state in which a composition resolves around a determinable attractor with its directional tensions in equilibrium. Strict phonemic palindrome instantiates this condition at the most legible scale. As stable forms expand, centered balance remains the governing principle: the attractor holds, the outer structure builds around it, the identity of the form remains readable from the center outward. The palindrome is not the definition of stability. It is what stability looks like at its simplest.

*ava* is not just the word for water. It is the most compressed accurate description of water possible in this language. Under any amount of additional compression, it will remain *ava*. It has already found its attractor state.

*oro* is not just the word for land. It is what land compresses to when you ask what land fundamentally is. Resonance at the center. Earth on both sides. The stone that vibrates.

*eye* is fire. Not the flames. Not the heat. Fire as the self recognizing itself. The collapse that produces awareness. Consciousness at its most irreducible.

*ili* is air. The element of connection and breath in its eternal form. The thread that persists through everything.

*umu* is voidlight. Depth at the center. The Black Sun. The interior that hums.

These five words are the pillars of the language. The most fundamental stable forms — not labels attached to things, but structural objects balanced around their own centers, already at their attractor states. The ones that cannot be compressed further because they are already at the bottom.

Everything else in the language is built from these foundations and the twenty-one forces that move through them. Every word, however long, can be read from its center outward and reveal the attractor state at its core. Every resolution unit, however complex, is a temporary arrangement of forces around stable centers.

And the act of reading this way, from the center outward rather than left to right, from the attractor state to the context, is itself a kind of practice. It trains a different kind of attention. Not sequential. Not linear. Radial. Centripetal. The kind of attention that looks for the fundamental before the contextual, the essential before the accidental, the true before the elaborate.

This is what the language means by **palindromic awareness**. Not the ability to spell words backward. The ability to read the world from the center outward. To find the attractor state of any experience and hold it before the descriptions accumulate around it.

The island before the word for island. The fire before the name for it. The self before the story of itself.

## VI. The Self in Five Letters

Before you encounter the full grammar of Varoli, before the morphemes and the verb system and the sentence structure, there is one thing worth understanding that will change how you read everything that follows.

If this section is all you take with you, then I have succeeded in the only way that has ever mattered to me as a poet, a linguist, a teacher, and a student of the mystic.

In English, the word I is the center of the universe.

Not philosophically. Grammatically. Every English sentence either has an I as its subject or implies one. The language is built around the individual perspective as the origin point of all meaning. Even when you are trying to describe something universal, something true beyond yourself, you say "I think" or "I believe" or "it seems to me." The grammar keeps dragging you back to the individual subject.

This is not a flaw. It is a fossil. English developed from cultures that centered the individual, that understood history as the story of particular people doing particular things, that built their philosophy around the primacy of personal experience. The grammar reflects those assumptions so seamlessly that most English speakers never question them. The I at the center of every sentence feels like the natural order of things because it has always been there.

In Varoli, "I" is *e*.

*e* is fire. The individual. Latin calls it "ego." The self-aware consciousness recognizing its own existence. The pilot light. Real, sacred, worth protecting. But *e* is one of five vowels. One perspective among five.

*i* in Varoli is not the self.

*i* is everything connected.

*i* is the universal field. The medium everything moves through. The omnidirectional permeating presence that contains all things. The air. The breath. The thing that was there before you and will be there after. Not the self. The everything. The closest word this language has to what some traditions call God, others call Source, and physics calls the field.

The English I and the Varoli *i* are not the same word. They sound identical. They could not be more different.

I know this because I felt it. Looking out my window toward a mountain in Tucson, I said *arova* to myself and imagined it as an island. The land became *o*. The desert light became *a*. The living things became *v*. And I became *e*, one fire in an enormous field, neither enlarged nor diminished by the recognition, just accurately placed. The entire scene, myself included, existing within the invisible but understood medium of *i*.

That is what Varoli offers anyone who learns it deeply enough. Not the erasure of the self but the accurate placing of it. *e* is real and worth protecting. But *e* is not *i*. The individual fire is not the field it burns in. The self is not the universe. And the language, if you let it, will keep reminding you of that distinction at every level of its structure, in every word you speak and hear and feel, until the reminder becomes unnecessary because the accurate proportion has become your cognitive default.

This is what makes Varoli not just a communication tool but a **linguistic instrument for consciousness**. The language does not impose a philosophical position. It encodes a more accurate map of reality than most languages offer and then lets you walk around inside it until the map becomes familiar and the territory becomes clear.

## VII. Yemu: The Unspoken Form

When you go underwater, you lose your voice.

This is not a metaphor. It is the origin story of Yemu.

Varoli is a spoken language. It is breath and rhythm and sound. It is the storyteller at the fire, the navigator calling out the reef, the diver surfacing and reporting what she found. It lives in the air. It requires a voice.
